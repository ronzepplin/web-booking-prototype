The Church Tour

The church tour is an integral part of the full experience and often serves as the starting point of the visit. Before ascending the tower, guests are invited to enter the prayer hall and become familiar with the spiritual and architectural heart of St. Nikolaus Church.

Visitors can take time to observe the interior architecture, its high vaulted ceilings, balanced proportions, and carefully guided light elements designed to create both clarity and reverence. Sitting quietly on the wooden benches allows guests to pause, slow down, and experience the atmosphere of the space as it has been shaped over centuries.

The tour also offers moments for reflection on the artworks, religious symbols, and relics found throughout the church. Each piece tells a story of faith, protection, and community life, helping visitors understand how the church interior and the tower together formed a place of worship, refuge, and identity for the city.

Observation

Arrive and take in the nave

The high vaults and long central aisle set the atmosphere. Visitors can orient themselves, notice the stained glass, and experience how sound and silence behave inside the space.

Contemplation

Artworks and devotional objects

The tour highlights selected artworks and relics. Many pieces reflect Baroque devotion and local history, shaped by centuries of faith, hardship, and restoration.

Understanding

Connect the church to the tower story

The interior is more than a beautiful hall: it explains why the tower mattered, connects the community, craftsmanship and the rhythm of liturgy and music.

Spotlight

A historic altar artwork watching over the city

The visitor will witness the oldest art work of Rosenheim, which is used as the current altarpiece. The historic work was produced in 1667 by Antonio Domenico Trivia who later became the court artist of Munich.  Behind the high altar hangs one of the oldest surviving artworks in Rosenheim. Created in the early 17th century, the painting shows Mary with the Christ Child, accompanied by St. Nicholas and St. Sebastian, overlooking the city of Rosenheim below. The artwork reflects the deep faith of the time and the belief in divine protection during an era marked by hardship, fire, and disease. Despite many reconstructions of the church, this painting has remained in its place for centuries and continues to watch over the city.

Details inside the prayer hall

The organ and the two-level interior

On the back side of the main prayer hall, the large organ is visible and mounted with the wall. The interior is divided into two stories: the lower floor is dedicated to prayer and liturgy, while the upper level accommodates musicians, singers, and the organ player.
