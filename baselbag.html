Baselbalg (the Bellow)

St. Nikolaus Church houses a large and powerful pipe organ, whose sound depends entirely on a continuous and precisely regulated supply of air. This air is produced by the Baselbalg (bellows), a traditional wooden mechanism that forms the silent foundation of the organ’s voice.

Unlike modern electric systems, the Baselbalg works through carefully engineered layers of wood that rise and fall slowly, storing and releasing air at a constant pressure. This gentle, rhythmic motion ensures that each pipe speaks evenly, preserving both pitch stability and tonal warmth.

Visible during the tower tour, the Baselbalg offers a rare insight into historic organ craftsmanship, reminding visitors that the grandeur of the organ’s sound is made possible by a quiet, almost invisible system working continuously in the background.

1) What the Baselbalg does

The Baselbalg provides a continuous, steady airflow that allows the organ to speak clearly. Without this stable air supply, the tone would be uneven and the sound would lose its character.

2) Built in wood, engineered for silence

The system is almost entirely wooden and consists of four stacked layers. These layers move slowly and quietly - rising and falling to generate the airflow needed for the organ pipes. This gentle mechanical motion helps preserve the organ’s traditional sound quality.

3) Organ concerts and community

St. Nikolaus Church regularly hosts organ concerts. The organ music is performed by an experienced musical team of 20 musicians and singers. Their performance includes classical numbers (especially J.S. Bach), choral works of 16th and 17th century and contemporary sacred music. Every Saturday at 12:00  an Organ concert is arranged regularly. Every one is welcome and entry is free. Thanks to the organizers. Due to the immense cost, donations are encouraged and appreciated.

4) Makers and craftsmanship

The large and magnificent organ was produced by Reil, a company from the Netherlands. It was installed in the church on 6th December (St. Nikolaus Day) of 2009. Since then its music has become an integral part of the Church liturgy. The chest organ in the chancel was built by a German company Linder from Nußdorf am inn.

A quiet system with a powerful effect

The Baselbalg is not dramatic or loud, yet it is very effective in producing the sound. Its steady, silent motion is a reminder that craftsmanship often works best when it stays in the background.
