The Bells

The bells are one of the most interesting elements of the tower tour. There are 7 bells in total. They are placed on the 9th floor. They are maintained by electronic motors which are programmed with a software to deliver the intended rhythm. All the bells can be functioned solely or together to create beautiful rhythmic sound.

The smallest bell Sterbeglocke with the highest tone (fs²) is also the oldest bell, built by Bartholomeus Wengle in Augsburg in 1606. The other 6 bells were built in 1947 by Czudnochowsky, Erding. The largest bell with the deepest tone(b⁰), Dreifaltigkeitsglocke or Trinity bell, weighs 3,100 kilograms.

Why elasticity matters

Due to a previous renovation mistake, the wooden bell frame (Glockenstuhl) was removed and replaced with concrete frame. This resulted in lack of elasticity, which is necessary for the bells.

Bells are heavy, and their swinging transfers force into the tower. A wooden bell frame provides elasticity and reduces stress on masonry. When rigid materials like concrete were used, it could not absorb the vibration and resulted in to creating cracks on the tower walls.

Reconstruction (1950-1951)

After some years of vibration and deterioration, another renovation took place. The upper tower (which was affected by the vibration) was dismantled and rebuilt. The wooden Glockenstuhl was restored instead of concrete to ensure elasticity. This is a marvellous example of how wood is superior to rigid materials in certain conditions.

The relic discovery

During the reconstruction of the tower in 1950, workers discovered a sealed metal box hidden inside the structure. The box, dated 1784, contained a relic of an unknown martyr, handwritten blessings, and prayers asking for protection of the church and the city.

After reconstruction, the contents were returned to the tower together with contemporary documents from 1950, continuing a long-standing tradition of safeguarding the church through faith and memory.
